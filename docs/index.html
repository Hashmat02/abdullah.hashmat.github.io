EMNLP 2025

Featured research contribution in multilingual bias evaluation for low-resource languages. Accepted at the Main Conference of EMNLP 2025 (Core: A*).

PakBBQ: A Culturally Adapted Bias Benchmark for QA

TL;DR: A culturally adapted question answering bias benchmark in English and Urdu for Pakistan, revealing language, cultural, and context-specific disparities in multilingual LLMs.

With the widespread adoption of Large Language Models (LLMs), it’s crucial to ensure fairness across all user communities. Most LLMs are trained on Western-centric data, neglecting low-resource languages and regional contexts. To address this, we introduce PakBBQ — an extension of the Bias Benchmark for Question Answering (BBQ) dataset — featuring 214 templates and 17,180 QA pairs in English and Urdu across 8 bias dimensions: age, disability, appearance, gender, socio-economic status, religion, regional affiliation, and language formality.

Our experiments show: (i) a 12% accuracy gain with disambiguation, (ii) stronger counter-bias behaviors in Urdu, and (iii) framing effects reducing stereotypes in negatively posed questions. This highlights the importance of contextualized benchmarks and simple prompt strategies for bias mitigation in low-resource settings.

2025 Senior Year Project

PakBBQ: Bias Benchmark

First culturally adapted QA benchmark for multilingual LLM bias evaluation in the Pakistani context.

Dataset of 17,180 QA examples in Urdu and English across 8 sociocultural bias categories. Disambiguated prompts boosted accuracy by ~12 points, and negatively framed questions reduced stereotypes across both languages. Cross linguistic gaps remained, with Urdu trailing English by 7–17 points due to data scarcity and higher prompt sensitivity.

2025 Independent Research Project

Bias Mitigation Knowledge Distillation

Knowledge distillation framework reducing inductive biases and improving generalization.

Achieved balanced bias (Shape: 0.49, Texture: 0.51) with confidence-aware KD, improving results on Stylized ImageNet and Canny Edge datasets over single-teacher and naive ensemble baselines. Base models showed strong bias tendencies (VGG-16: 81% texture, ViT: 59% shape)
